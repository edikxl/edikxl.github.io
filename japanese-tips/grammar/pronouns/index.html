Pronouns
1st Person
Both genders
私「わたくし」
For formal situations
私「わたし」
For normal polite situations
あっし
In the working class dialect of Tokyo Bay you also hear あっし, which ultimately comes from わたくし
お(い)ら
Are slang, non-vulgar, country-like variants
For getting plural add たち. Ex. 私→私たち, 僕→僕たち
Primarily Male
僕「ぼく」
From fairly polite to fairly casual situations. Is the general pronoun for guys of any age, but it is actually used by both genders in song. It may also be used to call for a boy child
俺「おれ」
A very rough version used almost exclusively by males in very casual situations. 俺 asserts masculinity and 俺様「おれさま」 is a very pompous variant
儂「わし」
This is casual and used by men over 50 and by old people in general in some regions. It is a contraction of わたし and sometimes written in 漢字 as 儂
Primarily Female
あた(く)し
A very feminine and casual way to refer to oneself. Many girls have decided to opt for わたしinstead because あたし has a cutesy and girly sound
うち
This is often used by women in casual and polite settings; however, there are many regions in Japan where this is a commonplace pronoun used by both genders
あたい
This is found in rougher casual speech of female speakers
Old-fashioned
我輩「わがはい」
Quite pompous
我「われ」
It is normally used in set expressions. The form 我が is used in formal situations to mean 'our'. Ex. 'our country' is 我が国.
朕「ちん」
Is equivalent to the 'royal we' used by the Emperor
2nd Person
Common
あなた
The most neutral. Is also an old-fashioned way for women to refer to their husband or lover
君「きみ」
Is a common casual 'you' and it can be used towards subordinates and peers. It is also used by men to their lovers. However, this last usage may not be acceptable by some women. It can be seen by some as belittling while by some as intimate. From guy to guy, it may be seen as rude
お前「お・まえ」
Are often used in vulgar situations by guys. However, they may still be seen used in just casual situations. They are actually extremely commonly used by men in close company as a general word to use with no such stigma
おめ
The plural form of you is あなたたち. However, when you should be polite to an audience, use みなさん(everyone)
Rude or old-fashioned
あんた
Is a rude variant in Standard Japanese when talking to someone, but it can be seen in casual situations. There are a lot of dialects in which it is the standard second person pronoun
手前「て・めえ」
Very rude. This is from てまえ but the non-contracted form is not used anymore in regular conversation. Like 「お前」, to add extra punch, people will usually say it like「てめ～～」
貴様「き・さま」
Very, very rude. Sounds like you want to take someone out
おのれ
Originally a first person reflexive pronoun meaning oneself, which it still means in set phrases
(お)ぬし
Not that common
われ
Most people don't use it aside from actual delinquent speech, which was the basis for pop-culture usage such as in movies and manga
It's important to note that 貴様 and お前 used to be very honorific terms, and the former stayed slightly honorific up until the early Edo Period. お主「おぬし」also has honorific origin
3rd Person
彼「かれ」
You can use 「彼」 and 「彼女」 for 'he' and 'she' respectively. Notice that 「彼」 and 「彼女」 can also mean 'boyfriend' and 'girlfriend'
彼女「かのじょ」
彼ら「かれら」
May refer to a group of people (they) with both men and women
彼女たち「かのじょたち」
Only refers to groups of women
あの人
Normal way of saying 'That person'
あの方
Formal way of saying 'That person'
あの男
That man
あの女
That women
For 'that woman' and 'that man', add の人 in formal contexts. Or, you may be inclined to say things like あの男性(の方)orあの女性(の方) for man and woman respectively
The pronoun 'it' is omitted because it is combined in Japanese with the concept of 'that', which is also a pronoun
